üìã The Power of Compound Interest

Compound interest is often called the eighth wonder of the world. It's the process where interest earned on an investment is reinvested to generate additional earnings over time. Unlike simple interest, which only earns on the principal amount, compound interest earns on both the principal and accumulated interest.

Albert Einstein reportedly said, "Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it; he who doesn't, pays it." Whether or not Einstein actually said this, the sentiment holds true - compound interest can work powerfully in your favor when investing, or against you when in debt.

üî¨ The Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) + PMT √ó (((1 + r/n)^(nt) - 1) / (r/n))

Where:
A = Final amount
P = Principal (initial investment)
r = Annual interest rate (decimal)
n = Number of times interest compounds per year
t = Number of years
PMT = Monthly contribution

üíº Example Scenarios

Starting Early: Investing $500/month from age 25 to 65 at 7% return = $1.3 million. Starting at 35 = $600k. That 10-year difference costs $700k!

The Rule of 72: Divide 72 by your interest rate to estimate doubling time. At 7%, money doubles every ~10 years. At 10%, every ~7 years.

‚ùì FAQ

What's a realistic interest rate to expect? ‚ñº
Historical stock market returns average 7-10% annually after inflation. Conservative estimates use 5-6%, while aggressive projections might use 8-10%. Savings accounts typically offer 0.5-4%, while bonds range from 2-6% depending on type and risk.
How often should interest compound for best results? ‚ñº
More frequent compounding is better, but the difference is small. Monthly vs annually might add 0.1-0.2% effective yield. Daily compounding provides minimal additional benefit over monthly. Focus more on the interest rate than compounding frequency.
Should I prioritize paying debt or investing? ‚ñº
Compare interest rates. Pay off high-interest debt (credit cards at 15%+) before investing. For low-interest debt (mortgages at 3-4%), investing often makes more mathematical sense. Consider both mathematical and psychological factors in your decision.
