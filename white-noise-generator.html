üìã Executive Summary: The Science of Sound Masking

White noise and similar ambient sounds work through a phenomenon called auditory masking - when one sound makes another sound less audible. By providing a consistent, predictable background sound, white noise effectively "fills in" the acoustic spectrum, making sudden noises less jarring and disruptive.

The human brain is wired to respond to changes in our environment. Sudden sounds trigger our fight-or-flight response, releasing cortisol and disrupting sleep, concentration, and relaxation. White noise creates a consistent acoustic baseline, reducing the contrast between background and foreground sounds.

Our White Noise Generator uses the Web Audio API to generate sounds directly in your browser - no audio files to download, no streaming required. ‚ùì Expert FAQ

Is white noise safe for sleeping? ‚ñº
Yes, research shows white noise is safe for sleep when used at moderate volumes. Keep the volume below 50 dB (about the volume of a quiet conversation) and place the source at least 3 feet from your bed. Some studies suggest it may even improve sleep quality.
Which sound is best for focus? ‚ñº
Pink noise is often preferred for focus work as it sounds more natural than white noise while still providing effective masking. Brown noise works well for those who find higher frequencies distracting. Experiment to find what works best for your ears and environment.
Can white noise damage hearing? ‚ñº
At normal listening volumes (under 50-60 dB), white noise does not damage hearing. However, prolonged exposure to any sound above 85 dB can cause hearing damage. Keep your volume at a comfortable, moderate level - you should be able to hold a conversation over it.
Will I become dependent on white noise? ‚ñº
While you may prefer sleeping with white noise once you're used to it, this isn't true dependence. You can sleep without it - it just might take a few nights to adjust. Many people find they can sleep with or without it after regular use.
